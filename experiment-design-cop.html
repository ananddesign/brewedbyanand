I Built a Plugin That Polices My Own Design System

Every design system eventually faces the same quiet problem: drift.

Not dramatic, not intentional, just small decisions made under deadline pressure. A colour that's close enough. A component that's basically the same. A text style that was manually overridden because the right one was three clicks away. Over time, these small deviations compound, and what was once a tight, intentional system starts feeling loose and inconsistent.

I've been there. And instead of adding another step to the review process, I built a plugin to handle it.

Meet Design Cop

Design Cop is a Figma plugin that audits your frames against your design system and surfaces style violations, detached colours, raw hex values, unlinked text styles, components that have drifted from their source. Think of it as a linter for your UI. The kind of tool that quietly catches what human eyes miss when you're three hours deep into a screen.

Version 2 levelled it up significantly. It doesn't just flag issues, it resolves them. If a layer is using a raw hex value that maps to an existing token in the DS, Design Cop auto-links it back. If a text layer's properties match a defined text style, it reconnects automatically. What used to be a tedious, manual cleanup, layer by layer, style by style, now happens in a single pass.

Why I built it

At Qween, we're in the middle of a full DS 2.0 rollout. Multiple screens being designed in parallel, across a small team, with fast-moving product decisions. Manual design system audits were becoming a real tax, something that should've been a quick check was turning into a 30-minute review process every time a new set of frames came in for critique.

I needed something that could catch inconsistencies before they reached handoff, not after. Something that worked inside the tool we were already using, without adding friction or context-switching.

Design Cop is that catch. It sits inside Figma, runs against your live frames, and gives you a clear picture of where the system is holding and where it's leaking.

What building it taught me

There's something clarifying about building tooling for your own workflow. It forces you to get specific — not just "we should follow the DS" but what exactly counts as a violation? What's intentional override vs. drift? Where do we draw the line? Writing those rules into code made me a more deliberate design system maintainer.

It also made me think differently about what design systems actually need. Good components are necessary but not sufficient. Without enforcement, a DS is just a suggestion. What makes a system stick isn't just quality of craft, it's how easy it is to stay inside the rails, and how fast you know when you've drifted outside them.

Design Cop is my answer to that. A small tool, built out of a real frustration, that quietly keeps the system honest.

What's next

I'm exploring ways to make the violation reports shareable, so reviews can happen asynchronously, with clear before/after states. Also thinking about how to extend it beyond style checks into spacing, layout grid adherence, and component prop mismatches.

If you're maintaining a design system for a fast-moving product, the audit problem is real. I'd love to hear how others are solving it.
